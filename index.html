A/B TESTING

00 Intro

The A/B Testing assignment involved using a website version A, modifying some parts of it in a version B to make improvements to the user flow, and then having participants test both version A and B to generate data about whether the changes made to B were successful. Given metrics like number of misclicks made, time spent on page, and amount of mouse movement, we could then analyze the data with various statistical analyses to see whether version B truly performed better in terms of allowing users to misclick less often and thus spend less time on the page and move their cursor around less.

01 Changing the Webpage

After using version A, I decided to restructure the page content so it would hopefully be easier to get a sense of the visual hierarchy with a more diverse set of font sizes and colors. Additionally, I also changed the color coding of the buttons to draw the users' eyes to the "Schedule Appointment" button, which is the one that the task required them to click.

02 Metric of Choice

In addition to two assigned metrics (time spent on page and whether the user misclicked while trying to accomplish the task) to analyze for our A/B testing, I chose mouse movement as my third, since it can indicate where the user might be scanning or how confused they might be depending on how much they move their mouse around on the page.

03 Hypotheses

Given these three metrics, I created pairs of null and alternate hypotheses to predict what the results would be if we compared version A with version B.

01 Misclick Rate

H0: There is no difference between the frequencies with which users click something else on the page before finding the correct button for the task in version A and version B.

HA: There is a lower frequency with which users click something else on the page before finding the correct button for the task in version B than there is in version A.

Reasoning for HA: The alternative hypothesis is based off of the design choice to make the “Make appointment” buttons bolder than the "See appointment" button to draw users' eye, so that users will misclick less often.

Prediction: I predict that I will end up rejecting the null hypothesis, since the purpose of the redesign was to make things easier to find on the page; as such, this should make misclicking less likely to happen if the design was successful.

02 Time Spent on Page

H0: Users using version A spend the same amount of time on the webpage as those using version B.

HA: Users using version A spend more time on the webpage than those using version B.

Reasoning for HA: The alternative hypothesis is based off of the new structure of the page, with lines to break up the sections and different font sizes/colors that should bring more visual hierarchy into the page and make the user flow quicker.

Prediction: I predict that I will end up rejecting the null hypothesis, since the point of the assignment was to make the entire page more efficient and easier to navigate; as such, this should make misclicking less likely to happen if the design was successful.

03 Mouse Movement

H0: Users using version A tend to move their cursor the same amount on the webpage as users using version B.

HA: Users using version A tend to move their cursor a different amount on the webpage compared to users using version B.

Reasoning for HA: The alternative hypothesis is based off of Version B’s more linear content structure and consistent but distinct style for different elements. Since the layouts differ, there’s no way to predict which results in less mouse movement, but we can still test to see if the layout changes the way people scan the page.

Prediction: I predict that I will end up rejecting the null hypothesis since the new webpage should organize the information in a way that’s easier to scan, which should allow users to move their cursor around less before finding what they need to make the appointment.

04 Statistical Analysis (Misclick Rate)

For the misclick rate, I chose a chi2 test with the two categories being “misclicked” and “didn’t misclick,” since we are really only measuring those two categories (based on true vs false) rather than measuring something on a continuous scale.

After looking at the data, I fail to reject the null hypothesis because p > 0.05. We know this because our p-value is approximately 0.775, which is much larger than our alpha of 0.05 and thus is not statistically significant. With a chi2 value of about 0.081, however, we can see that the order of magnitude in terms of the difference between the two samples is not too big. Lastly, our degrees of freedom (1) mainly comes from there being two categories and two samples that we compare.

05 Statistical Analysis (Time Spent on Page)

For time spent on page, I conducted a one-sided T test with A > B, since we only want to know if A > B, not if A != B, and our data is continuous.

After the analysis, I found statistically significant evidence that the alternative hypothesis is true because p <= 0.05. Our calculated p-value was approximately 0.001, which is much smaller than our alpha of 0.05 and is thus statistically significant. With a t-score of about -3.628, we can see that the order of magnitude in terms of the difference between the two samples is a sizable amount (though not extreme), and our degrees of freedom of about 22.880 comes from there being on average around 22-23 participants.

06 Statistical Analysis (Mouse Movement)

For mouse movement, I performed a two-sided T test with A != B, since we’re only concerned with whether there’s a difference in mouse movement on a continuous scale, not necessarily with whether that difference is positive or negative.

After calculating our values, I found statistically significant evidence that the alternative hypothesis is true because p <= 0.05. Our calculated p-value was approximately 0.000, which is much smaller than our alpha of 0.05 and is thus statistically significant. With a t-score of about -4.221, we can see that the order of magnitude in terms of the difference between the two samples is a decent amount, and our degrees of freedom of around 22.896 comes from there being around 22-23 participants on average.

07 Summary Statistics

In general, my data was pretty varied, with the means for time on page comparing samples A/B to be around 22000/10000, the medians being 20000/10000, and the ranges being 60000/7000; the means for mouse movement comparing samples B/A to be around 6471/2500, the medians being 5500/2000, and the ranges being 17000/1700.

For both of the continuous categories (those just mentioned), B tended to have lower means and medians, suggesting that the process was sped up by the changes; however, this could also have been affected by the users’ experience in navigating the page by the time they used version B, since the volatility of the data (suggested by range) is much smaller. For the categorical data, the expected values of around 6/6 for misclicks in samples A/B, as well as those of around 17/14 for those without misclicks, is pretty close to the actual results for those categories.

Overall, I collected more data points for sample B (23) than sample A (20), but because there are only three more participants, it’s unlikely to make an extreme difference in the results.
